Introduction

Yoga is the unification of the microcosm with the macrocosmos

Hari-Yoga is a yoga-system created by ████████ (website author). It is comprised of three limbs of practice.

  1. Pranayama
  2. Pratyahara
  3. Dhyana

Pranayama and Pratyahara are supports to the practice of Dhyana. Samadhi (realisation) is achieved via correct practice of Dhyana. Pranayama is an external practice, whereas Pratyahara and Dhyana are internal practices. Perform them in order.

  • Right Breath Control
  • Right Introspection (Withdrawing Inwards)
  • Right Concentration

These 3 lead to Samadhi.

Three Rules of Practice
  1. Do not force, do not concentrate, just be aware.
  2. No creative visualisation, no imagination, just awareness.
    • Never try to visualise or imagine anything. If images, lights, spiritual beings or anything else comes to your vision, that is fine. But don't make them up, don't try to induce them. Do not actively visualise any pattern into your field of consciousness.
  3. Do not analyse during an experience.
    • Do not try to analyse as soon as something happens. Otherwise you will lose your perception immediately, because you will be caught straight back into the discursive mind.

1 — Pranayama

Purification or Clearing of the Nadis

— Pranayama Instructions
— Three Signs of Nadisuddhi

Pranayama (breath-control) cleanses the mind and body by clearing the nadis (energy channels).

Pranayama Instructions

Nadi Shodana
Alternating Nostril Breathing Warning: If you feel uncomfortable while practicing this exercise then stop immediately.
  • Assume a comfortable Posture
  • First Round: Inhaling through Left Nostril
    1. Blocking the right nostril with the thumb.
      Inhale (7s) air though the left nostril filling the chest and abdomen.
    2. Hold the breath for as long as possible (~21s) without strain, while fixing the mind on the vital fire that burns in the Nabhi centre (2 fingerwidths below the navel) and the Mula centre (the perineum).
    3. Blocking the left nostril with the middle finger.
      Exhale slowly (7s) through the right nostril.
  • Second Round: Inhaling through Right Nostril
    1. While blocking the left nostril with the middle finger,
      Inhale (7sec) air though the right nostril filling the chest and abdomen.
    2. Hold the breath for as long as possible (~21s) without strain, while fixing the mind on the vital fire that burns in the Nabhi centre (2 fingerwidths below the navel) and the Mula centre (the perineum).
    3. Blocking the right nostril with the thumb.
      Exhale slowly (7sec) through the left nostril.
  • Repeat 2 more times for one complete exercise of Nadi Shodana
[ Note: These instructions are for right-handed people, swap middle finger and thumb for the left hand. ]

The 3 Signs of Nadisuddhi

The purity of the nadis (nadisuddhi) is indicated by certain signs:

  1. Lightness of the body
  2. Enhancement of the metabolism and power of digestion
  3. Hearing spiritial sounds (nada)

One should continue to practice until these signs permit its abandonment.

2 — Pratyahara

Introspection – Withdrawing Inwards

— The 8 Marma Points
— Pratyahara Instructions

Pratyahara is the greatest help to yoga. A knowledge of the eight vital points (marmas) is essential to the success of yoga. Pratyahara is the practice of drawing the prana from point to point. It allows the mind to withdraw inwards—like the turtle retracting into its shell.

The 8 Marma Centres – Inner Points

Marmas are internal pressure points activated by drawing prana into them.

The 8 Marma Points — Vital Centres

Name Position Chakra Nadi
1 Murddha Top of Head (Bregma) Sahasrara (crown-chakra) Sushumna
2 Lalata Centre of Forehead Rel. to Sahasrara
3 Sthapani/Ajna Middle of Eyebrows Ajña (brow-chakra)
4 Nasamula Root of the Nose (top of nose) Rel. to Ajña ?
5 Nila Base of the Throat Vishuddhi ?
6 Hridaya Centre of the Heart/Chest Anahata Varuna
7 Nabhi 2 Finger-widths below centre of the Navel Manipura Vishvodhara
8 Mula Perineum (middle of body) Muladhara Alambusha

Pratyahara Instructions

Marmasthana
Residing in the Marmas
  • Assume a comfortable Posture
  • Employ Ujjayi throughout this exercise
  • First Round: Down the Body
      • Inhale (7s) prana into the Murddha.
      • Hold (1s) the breath, retaining prana in the point.
      • Exhale (7s) while focusing on the point.
      • Pause (1s).
      • Inhale (7sec) and draw prana into the Lalata.
      • Hold (7sec) the breath and retain prana in the point.
      • Exhale (7sec) while focusing on the point.
      • Pause (1s).
    3. Continue drawing down prana through the rest of the points
  • Second Round: Up the Body
      • Inhale (7s) prana up into the Nabhi.
      • Hold (1s) the breath, retaining prana in the point.
      • Exhale (7s) while focusing on the point.
      • Pause (1s).
      • Inhale (7sec) and draw up prana into the Hridaya.
      • Hold (7sec) the breath and retain prana in the point.
      • Exhale (7sec) while focusing on the point.
      • Pause (1s).
    3. Continue drawing up prana through the rest of the points
  • Repeat 2 more times for one complete exercise of Marmasthana

3 — Dhyana

Concentration or Absorption

— Nirguna-Dhyana
— Saguna-Dhyana
— Benefits of Dhyana

Dhyana (meditation) alone is the cause of bondage or liberation. For whatever a man identifies with—he becomes. Dhyana is to realise the Atman (Self) through the mind. This is done in two ways; either abstract meditation devoid of attributes (nirguna) or concrete meditation with attributes (saguna). There are many techniques of Saguna-Dhyana that can be employed, whereas abstract Nirguna-Dhyana has only one—which is the highest and principal technique. The prerequisite for Dhyana is mastery of the marmas.

Nirguna — Formless Meditation

Dhyana without Form

The constant mental conviction:

"I am Brahman"

I am Brahman—one, eternal, effulgent, without beginning or end, all glorious, all purity/pure, all-pervading like space, endless, unmanifest, immovable, steady, fixed, unsurpassable, great and small, gross and subtle, formless.

Not comprehensible by sight, beyond touch, without smell or taste, beyond sense-perception, incomprehensible, not even in the form of akasa, free from change, unequalled, all bliss, perishable, eternal, withiut beginning, ever new, neither being nor not-being, the cause of all causes, the basis of everything, one with the cosmos, pervading the entire world, formless, without parts, seen and unseen, both manifest and unmanifest, present within and without, inside and out, existing in all forms, everywhere, omnipresent.

Saguna — Meditations on Form or Concrete Absorption

Dhyana with Form

Ajna-Dhyana
Contemplation (Dhyana) on one's own Self in space between the eyebrows (Ajna)

The highest knowledge (jnana, gnosis) is the conviction: "I am That which is Atman"—which is all light, the cause of all, pervading the whole body and universe, shining with indescribable and immeasurable splendour.

Seated steadily with the body relaxed, one must meditate upon the Divine* in the center of the eyebrows, with the intention of uniting with All-Śiva—who is free from all change, the highest Atma, all peace, all powerful, effulgent, immutable, imperishable, all light, immortal.

*Imagining yourself to be one with it.

Focus the prana/breath* in the centre of the eyebrows (Ajna) through the sushumna, fix it there under contemplation until the mind is totally absorbed and lost in it.

*Employ Ujjayi.

See with the mind in the centre of the eyebrows, the Divine, that is effulgent, the source of everything, the basis of the world, causeless and of immeasurable splendour. The realisation that “He is I (the self)” is the most praiseworthy among many kinds of dhyana.

The Benefits of Dhyana

Six months of Dhyana conquers death. In one year, one becomes completely liberated—even while living. One who has attained such freedom is never again afflicted by evil or misery. It is impossible to describe the state of happiness within reach for one who has attain. They are eternally free.

Innumerable types of dhyana have been described. The most princiapl forms have been described above. Whether with form or without form, one may realise the Self within themselves, and attain the state of samadhi. Give up the desire for the results of your worldly acts, and devote yourself to the practice of right dhyana at all times.

Posture (Asana)
  • Posture
    • Choose a soft seat.
    • Assume a comfortable and steady posture.
      • Full Lotus (Padmasana): Sit with both feet placed on the opposite thighs.
      • Half Lotus (Ardha Padmasana): Sit with one foot placed on the opposite thigh and the other foot resting on the floor or beneath the opposite leg.
      • Cross-Legged (Sukhasana): Sit with legs crossed naturally.
      • Alternative: Sitting in a hard-backed chair is suitabme if other postures are unattainable.
    • Keep the spine vertical, with the neck and head in alignment.
    • Sit facing the East or the North.
    • Remain steady—without any movement.
    • Only assume a posture that you have well-mastered.
  • Only breath through Nostrils
    • The mouth is to be kept closed; do not allow air to enter or escape by way of the mouth. Always breath through the nostrils (in the exercises and in daily life).
  • Eyes
    • If closed: look up as far as possible without strain or discomfort. (Shambavi Mudra)
    • If open: fixed upon the tip of the nose. (Nasikagra)
  • Hands — Place the right hand on the left hand.
  • Place — In an agreeable and uninhabited place, protected against heat and cold.
  • Bathing — Bathe before practice. Scrub the skin to open the pores, which is conducive to absorbing prana.
  • Attitude
    • Perform the practices after becoming pure and focused.
    • Learn to keep silent. Keep your progress and experiences secret.
    • Try to reign in your emotions, excessive expression of emotions drains your energy. Keep the face soft—try not to tense the face-muscles.
Ujjayi Breath Vibration in the Larynx or Throat Friction

Ujjayi Breathing is breathing with friction in the lower part of the back of throat. It energises the larynx centre (Nila).

Practice by: focusing on the spot in the lower-back part of the throat. If you notice, you can control where the 'crest' of your breath lands. Normally, it crests at the roof of the mouth as it travels back into the throat. This 'crest' must be placed on the larynx. This will create a 'wind' type of sound. The larynx will then subtly vibrate—a sign of activation of the Nila centre.

Do not try to make it perfect, just position the friction there.

Daily Regimen The Three Limbs (Pranayama, Pratyahara, Dhyana) should be practiced in solitude six times a day, three times per morning, afternoon, and evening period. The practice of Hari-Yoga should be continued according to necessity for three to four months or three to four years.
Yogic Diet & Fasting

A Yogic or Sattvic Diet is a plant-based diet within Ayurveda, where food is divided into three qualities (gunas):

  • Tamasic — foods that decrease energy
  • Rajasic — foods that increase energy
  • Sattvic — balanced, pure, natural, clean, vitalising, essential foods
    • Based on the principle of non-violence (ahimsa)—of not causing harm to other living beings

A sattvic diet is a places emphasis on seasonal foods, fruits (if one has no sugar issues), nuts, seeds, oils, ripe vegetables, legumes, whole grains, and non-meat based proteins. It recommends that one must eat only when one feels hungry and neither overeat nor eat to completely fill the capacity of one’s stomach.

Guna Foods
Sattva
(natural & balanced foods)
  • Fruits should be consumed every day (fresh and organic preferred to frozen or preserved)
  • Dairy products include:
    • yogurt, cheese (paneer), fresh and raw milk and butter, ghee (can be aged forever)
    • Freshness is key with dairy
    • Milk that is not consumed fresh can be refrigerated for one to two days in its raw state, but must be brought to a boil before drinking, and drunk while still warm.
  • Most mild vegetables are sattvic
    • Pungent vegetables are tamasic
      • leek, garlic, onion
    • Alliums are not sattvic
      • garlic, onion, leeks, shallots
    • Fungus is not sattvic
      • yeasts, molds, mushrooms
  • Whole grains include:
    • organic rice, whole wheat, spelt, oatmeal, barley
    • Yeasted breads are not recommended—unless toasted.
    • Grains can be lightly roasted before use in cooking to remove some of their heavy quality.
    • Wheat and other grains can be sprouted before cooking to increase their nutritional content.
  • Legumes include:
    • mung beans, lentils, yellow split peas, chickpeas, aduki beans, common beans, bean sprouts
    • In general: the smaller the bean—the easier to digest
  • Nuts and seeds
  • Herbs include:
    • ashwagandha, bacopa, calamus, gotu, kola, ginkgo, jatamansi, purnarnava, shatavari, saffron, shankhapushpi, tulsi, rose
  • Sattvic spices are basil and coriander.
    • All other spices are considered either rajasic or tamasic.
    • Although, over time, certain sects have classified a few spices as sattvic. Use in small amounts. These included:
      • cardamom, cinnamon, cumin, fennel, fenugreek, black pepper, fresh ginger, turmeric
    • Rajasic spices like red pepper are normally excluded, but can be used in small amounts to clear channels blocked by mucus and to counter tamas.
  • Sweeteners one may use:
    • raw honey, jaggery, palm jaggery, raw sugar (not refined), coconut palm sugar, stevia or stevia leaf
    • In stricter traditions sugar, honey, along with all other sweeteners are excluded
Rajas
(stimulating foods) 		Rajasic food may lead to sadness and illness.

    • spicy

    • acidic
  • Also considered Rajasic:
    • junk food

Tamas
(sedative foods)

Foods which are harmful to both mind and body. Mental harm means a duller less subtle consciousness. Bodily harm means stress to any organ—indirectly or directly.

  • Tamasic foods include:
    • meat, fish, eggs ▪︎ garlic, onion, scallion, leek, chive, mushroom ▪︎ alcohol, opium ▪︎ durian fruit ▪︎ blue cheese
    • stale food (or food left out for more than 3 hours)

Fasting

For the organism to be a true vessel for higher energies—it must be free of any waste. You must eat less and smaller if you want to prepare your body and spirit for increased activity. The over-fed fall asleep easily, become weary and degenerate. On the other hand, those who fast alert, awake, and diligent. Do not fear hunger, it is a ascending ladder of development.

However, when it comes to any kind of fasting, there are limits which cannot be violated if you want to avoid harm. These limits express themselves in your subjective state of health. under no circumstances should you allow yourself to be weakened, emaciated or reduced to misery.

All these instructions in regards to food must be fulfilled with an inner preparedness, not something to which you subject yourself unwillingly.
